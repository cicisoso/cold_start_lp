\title{Tail Nodes Benefit Most from Graph Self-Supervision \\ in Link Prediction}

\author{Anonymous}
\affiliation{\institution{Anonymous Institution}\country{}}

\begin{abstract}
Graph self-supervised learning (SSL), particularly contrastive learning (CL), has become a standard technique for improving link prediction in graph neural networks. However, prior work evaluates these methods using aggregate metrics that obscure where the improvements actually occur. In this paper, we present a systematic, degree-stratified analysis of graph SSL for link prediction across 6 datasets, 3 GNN backbones (GCN, GraphSAGE, GAT), and 8 training methods. Our key findings are: (1)~SSL gains are disproportionately concentrated on low-degree (tail) edges, with improvements of up to 13.6\% AUC for the lowest-degree edges compared to 3.3\% for high-degree edges---a pattern consistent across AUC, AP, and Hits@K metrics; (2)~partial correlation analysis confirms that degree independently predicts SSL gain after controlling for clustering coefficient and neighborhood diversity; (3)~the benefit decomposes into augmentation regularization and the contrastive objective, with their relative importance depending on graph sparsity; (4)~the SSL benefit extends to inductive cold-start settings, with CL improving unseen-node link prediction by 3.8--11.3\% AUC; and (5)~selective CL targeting low-degree nodes does not outperform global CL. These findings provide actionable guidance for practitioners and challenge the implicit assumption that SSL improvements are uniformly distributed across the graph.
\end{abstract}
